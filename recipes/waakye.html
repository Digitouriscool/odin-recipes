Waakye

Description

Waakye is a traditional Ghanaian dish made by cooking rice together with beans, usually black-eyed peas, in a single pot. The rice and beans are simmered with dried sorghum leaves, which give the dish its distinctive reddish-brown color and a subtle earthy flavor. This method of cooking allows the grains of rice and the beans to absorb the same seasoned broth, creating a hearty and satisfying base that is both filling and flavorful.

What makes waakye especially unique is the variety of accompaniments typically served with it. The dish is often paired with sides such as fried plantains, boiled eggs, spaghetti, gari (grated cassava), shito (a spicy black pepper sauce), and different types of meat or fish. These toppings add layers of texture and flavor, turning a simple rice-and-beans dish into a rich and diverse meal. Vendors and home cooks often customize the combination of sides depending on personal preference or regional style.

Waakye is commonly eaten as a breakfast or lunch meal in Ghana and is widely sold by street vendors across the country. It holds a strong cultural presence and is a staple comfort food for many Ghanaians. The dish is appreciated not only for its taste but also for its affordability and ability to bring together a variety of flavors in one plate.

Ingredients

  • 1 ½ cups black eyed peas, washed and drained
  • 3-4 long sorghum leaves/waakye leaves, washed and cut in half (traditionally used, but optional)
  • ¼ tsp baking soda
  • 2 cups of jasmine rice
  • ¾ tsp salt
  • 1-2 Tbsp coconut oil, organic, cold pressed (optional)/li>

Steps

  1. In a pot add washed beans, 2 cups of water, sorghum leaves (if using) and baking soda. Cover your beans and bring to boil then reduce to simmer and cook for about an hour or until beans are tender.

  2. Around the 30-minute mark, top up beans with 2 more cups of water once the water has almost evaporated.

  3. After the beans are tender and fully cooked, remove the sorghum leaves, if used.

  4. Rinse jasmine rice with water until it runs almost clear.

  5. Add rice to beans and top with ¾-1 cup of water. Add salt. The amount of water you add just depends on how much water evaporated while cooking your beans, it is better to start with slightly less water and add a bit more later if necessary.

  6. Bring rice and bean mixture to a light boil over medium heat, then reduce to very low, cover and let cook for 30 minutes, stirring occasionally.

  7. Cover with parchment paper and pot lid and continue to cook for 10 minutes.

  8. If rice seems underdone, but not all the water has evaporated, just cover and continue cooking on low. If the rice seems under done but it is very dry, add ¼ cup of water, cover, and keep cooking in 10 minute increments on very low until done.

  9. Stir coconut oil into waakye (this is optional but add nice flavour) and serve.
